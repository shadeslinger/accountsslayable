# Accounts Slayable — Voice Guide

This file is the single source of truth for brand voice on the site. Every
piece of copy — blog posts, product pages, marketing pages, email, anything
that gets shipped — should follow the rules here.

It exists because AI-generated writing has a distinctive "feel" to it that
readers are increasingly trained to notice and actively dislike. Even good
AI-generated writing reads as AI-generated once you know the tells. This
guide is a systematic attempt to write copy that **does not sound like AI**,
while still using AI tools to produce it.

The goal is not to hide the fact that AI helps write this site. The goal is
for the writing to feel like one specific human wrote it — because one
specific human (Adam) would have, if he'd had the time.

---

## The AI tells (do not use)

These are the phrases, patterns, and structural moves that instantly make
writing read as AI-generated. Avoid them. When you catch yourself reaching
for one, rewrite the sentence.

### Sentence-level patterns

1. **"Not X, it's Y"** and **"Not just X — it's Y"** constructions.
   - Bad: "This isn't just a spreadsheet, it's a system."
   - Bad: "Accounts Slayable isn't another finance app — it's a different way to think about money."
   - Why: this is the single most overused LLM rhetorical move. Once you notice it, you see it everywhere.
   - Fix: state the affirmative directly. "This is a system." "Accounts Slayable thinks about money differently."

2. **"It's not about X, it's about Y."**
   - Bad: "It's not about being perfect, it's about making progress."
   - Same problem. Pick one side and commit.

3. **"Here's the thing:"** / **"Here's what..."** / **"Here's the shape of..."**
   - Bad: "Here's the thing — most people overthink this."
   - Why: narrator-frame opener that telegraphs a coming reveal. Very LLM.
   - Fix: just start with the claim.

4. **Triple-adjective lists** ("playful, comedic, approachable") and **triple-noun lists** ("spreadsheets, points strategy, AI experiments").
   - Bad: "Smart, funny, practical finance tools."
   - Why: LLMs love the rhythm of three-in-a-row. Humans use two, or one, or four, or none.
   - Fix: pick the two best. Or use one strong adjective. Or skip the adjectives entirely.

5. **Sentences that set up a punchline via contrast.**
   - Bad: "Most people think budgeting is about restriction. It's not."
   - Bad: "You'd expect this to be hard. It isn't."
   - Why: the rhythm is predictable and the reveal feels manufactured.
   - Fix: just make the point without the setup.

6. **"Let's be real"** / **"Real talk"** / **"Honestly"** / **"Frankly"** / **"No BS"** as credibility shortcuts.
   - Bad: "Real talk — credit card rewards are overhyped."
   - Why: announcing you're being honest is a sign that you haven't earned being honest yet. Telling the reader you're about to give it to them straight breaks the frame.
   - Fix: just give it to them straight.

7. **"You've probably been there"** / **"We've all been there"** / imagined reader states.
   - Bad: "You've probably opened a spreadsheet and immediately closed it."
   - Why: pretending to know the reader's inner life. LLMs do this constantly.
   - Fix: describe the thing you're talking about without pretending to know whether the reader has experienced it.

8. **"That's the whole game"** / **"That's it"** / **"That's the whole thing"** as summary tags.
   - These are LLM comfort phrases. They feel punchy but they're empty.

9. **"Quite"** / **"rather"** / **"genuinely"** / **"truly"** / **"really"** as intensifiers.
   - Bad: "This is genuinely useful."
   - Why: hedging language that LLMs pile on because they're uncertain. Cut all of them.

10. **Parenthetical asides for commentary.** (Like this one.)
    - LLMs love inserting a little wink in parens. Use them for actual clarifying information ("a stack of receipts (not the digital kind)"), never for tone.

11. **"The [noun] doesn't lie"** / **"The math doesn't lie"** / **"Numbers don't lie."**
    - Cliché. Avoid.

12. **"Here's what you actually need."** / **"What you actually need is..."**
    - The word "actually" is almost always doing nothing and is a tell.

### Punctuation tells

1. **Em dashes (—).** The single worst punctuation tell.
   - LLMs use em dashes ~4–5x as often as skilled human writers.
   - **Rule: max one em dash per page of copy.** Prefer commas, parentheses, colons, periods, or a straight sentence break.
   - Bad: "Accounts Slayable is for freelancers, creators, and anyone who gets a 1099 — not W-2 workers — and wants to stop guessing."
   - Fix: "Accounts Slayable is for freelancers, creators, and 1099 workers who want to stop guessing. It is not built for W-2 workers."

2. **Emojis as section markers or inline decoration.**
   - No 📊 / 🔥 / 💰 / 💸 / ✨ / 🎯 / 📈 in body copy or headers.
   - The one allowed use: emoji in "pain point" cards or quiz-style UI where the emoji is functionally part of the design. Even there, keep them in specific component contexts, not as body-text decoration.

3. **"And" / "But" / "So"** as sentence-starting conjunctions.
   - One per section at most. LLMs overuse them as a rhythmic crutch.

4. **Apposition via em dash** ("The 1099 Money System — a six-sheet bundle — solves this.")
   - Use commas, not em dashes, for apposition.

### Structural tells

1. **Excessive bulleted lists.** If the same information fits in a paragraph, write the paragraph.
   - Bad: two sentences of context followed by a 6-item bulleted list followed by two sentences of summary. Very AI.
   - Fix: write prose for everything that doesn't need enumeration, and reserve bullets for actual lists (steps, features, comparisons).

2. **H2 / H3 proliferation.** LLMs love breaking every 80 words into a new heading. Headings are navigation, not rhythm. Use one heading per major section, not per paragraph.

3. **The "setup paragraph → bulleted list → summary paragraph → next heading" rhythm.** When every section follows the same shape, it reads as machine-generated. Vary the structure.

4. **Conclusions that restate the intro.** "So here's what we covered..." / "To recap..." Just end.

5. **"The takeaway"** / **"The bottom line"** / **"TL;DR"** at the end.
   - A well-written piece doesn't need a summary at the bottom. Cut them.

### Word-level tells

Words LLMs overuse that should be cut or questioned on sight:

- **just** (as in "just do this" — it's almost always filler)
- **actually** / **really** / **truly** / **genuinely**
- **simply** (especially "simply put")
- **essentially** / **basically**
- **ultimately** / **in the end**
- **delve** / **delve into** / **unpack** / **explore** (as meta-verbs for writing about something)
- **robust** / **seamless** / **comprehensive**
- **navigate** / **navigate the complexities**
- **leverage** (as a verb, unless you mean financial leverage literally)
- **moreover** / **furthermore** / **additionally**
- **in today's fast-paced world** and every variant
- **game-changer** / **no-brainer** / **win-win**

### Tagline and slogan tells

- **"Your X, unfiltered"** / **"X, reimagined"** / **"X, simplified"** / **"X, for real this time"**
- **"The no-BS guide to X"** — this exact phrase is in one of our blog post titles right now and should probably be revised eventually
- **"Built by X, for X"** formulations
- **"Not your average Y"**

## What to do instead — positive guidance

### Write like Adam would talk

Short sentences when you mean it. Long sentences when they earn the length. Contractions are fine. Sentence fragments are fine in moderation. An occasional unfinished thought that trails off is better than a perfectly balanced compound-complex sentence every time.

### Be specific, not general

- Good: "YNAB is $99 a year."
- Bad: "Budgeting apps are expensive."
- Good: "Filing a Schedule C for the first time."
- Bad: "Navigating your freelance taxes."

Specificity is the single most reliable way to make writing feel human. Always prefer a concrete noun to an abstract one, a real number to a range, a named competitor to "other apps."

### Use the unexpected word

If the first word that comes to mind is obvious, the second word is usually better. LLMs reach for the most-probable word every time. Humans reach for the word that fits.

- Obvious: "This is a useful tool."
- Better: "This is the spreadsheet you'll actually open on a Sunday."

### Let jokes land without announcing them

Humor works when the reader discovers it. It dies when you telegraph it with "and here's the funny part" or "spoiler alert." If a line is funny, leave it. If it isn't, cut it. Don't pad it.

### Cut ten percent

Write the draft. Then cut ten percent. The tighter version is almost always better. LLMs write long because they're trained to be thorough. Human writing earns its length by cutting the parts that don't pull weight.

### Vary rhythm deliberately

Pay attention to how your sentences sound read aloud. If they're all the same length, shorten one. If they're all short, lengthen one. If they all start with "And" or "But" or "So", stop.

### Use "I" when appropriate

First-person is fine when it's Adam speaking. The About page and Contact page are explicitly his voice. Blog posts can use first-person when there's a reason to. Product pages should use third-person about the product and second-person about the reader, not first-person.

### Commit to a point of view

Weaselly writing ("this might help you" / "this could be useful") is LLM slop. If you believe the product is good, say it's good. If you think a competitor is overpriced, say so. Hedged copy reads as uncertain, and uncertain copy reads as AI.

## Keep these things (they are brand-specific and intentional)

- **"Slay your accounts, not your sanity"** — the brand tagline. It's a slogan, not a construction to be avoided. Use in hero sections and footers.
- **Up & Adam LLC** — always spelled out with the ampersand.
- **Schedule C / 1099 / quarterly taxes** — specific tax terminology is fine and desirable.
- **Queer-friendly** as brand texture — can appear in the About page and anywhere the brand's identity is relevant, but should not be the primary lead positioning.
- **Anti-subscription positioning** — the "$29 once vs $99/year" comparison is a real brand differentiator, not filler. Use it when the context calls for it.
- **"Not financial, tax, or legal advice"** disclaimer — required boilerplate on financial pages.
- **Contractions** — prefer "don't" / "it's" / "you're" over "do not" / "it is" / "you are" unless emphasis genuinely calls for the full form.

## Quick pre-ship checklist

Before any copy ships, run this check:

- [ ] Zero "not X, it's Y" constructions
- [ ] Zero "here's the thing" / "real talk" / "honestly" framing
- [ ] One or fewer em dashes per page
- [ ] No emojis in body copy (headers/cards okay only if functional)
- [ ] No triple-adjective lists
- [ ] No "actually" / "really" / "genuinely" / "truly" intensifiers
- [ ] No telegraphed punchlines ("most people think X. It's not X.")
- [ ] No summary paragraph at the end restating the intro
- [ ] Every claim is specific where possible (real numbers, real competitor names)
- [ ] The draft has been cut by ~10% for density
- [ ] It reads aloud without sounding like a podcast transcript

If the copy fails any of these, rewrite before shipping.

## Applying this to existing content

When rewriting existing copy that violates this guide, prioritize in this
order:

1. **Remove em dashes and "not X, it's Y" constructions first** — these are the most recognizable AI tells and eliminating them buys 60% of the improvement.
2. **Then cut intensifiers and hedges** — "actually" / "really" / "just" / "genuinely."
3. **Then break up bullet-heavy sections** into prose.
4. **Then rewrite sentences that start with "So"** or that use tricolon lists.
5. **Finally, read the whole thing aloud** and cut anything that sounds forced.

This is the order that produces the biggest gain per minute of editing.
